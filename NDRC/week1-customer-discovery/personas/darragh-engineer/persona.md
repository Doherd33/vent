# Persona: Darragh — Associate Engineer (Manufacturing Support)

## Demographics
- **Role:** Associate Engineer — Manufacturing Sciences & Technology (MS&T)
- **Experience:** 6 years in biopharma (started as operator, moved to engineering)
- **Employer:** Biopharma CDMO, Ireland
- **Shift pattern:** Days (Mon–Fri) but on-call for manufacturing investigations

---

## Day-to-Day

Darragh sits between the floor and the office. He's the bridge between operators who run the process and the quality/regulatory teams who govern it. His day involves:
- Investigating deviations and writing root cause analyses
- Authoring and revising SOPs
- Reviewing batch records for completeness and errors
- Supporting tech transfers and process improvements
- Analysing process data for trends and excursions
- Providing on-floor engineering support when things go wrong
- Writing CAPA effectiveness checks
- Attending cross-functional meetings (quality, ops, validation, regulatory)

## Frustrations

### 1. "I spend 80% of my time writing about the process and 20% actually improving it"
The paperwork burden is crushing. A single deviation investigation can take 15–30 hours of documentation: gathering evidence, interviewing operators, pulling process data, writing the root cause, getting it reviewed, implementing the CAPA, writing the effectiveness check. He became an engineer to solve problems, not to write reports about problems.

### 2. "I can't connect the dots across systems"
An investigation requires pulling data from the QMS (deviation history), the historian (process data), the MES (batch record entries), the CMMS (maintenance logs), and the LMS (training records). None of these systems talk to each other. He manually cross-references across 4-5 platforms to piece together what happened. Every investigation starts with an hour of data gathering.

### 3. "The same deviations keep happening and nobody sees the pattern"
He knows that Bioreactor 3 has had the same pH probe drift issue three times in six months. But that pattern only exists in his head — the QMS treats each deviation as an isolated event. There's no system showing: "This equipment has had X deviations of Y type in Z timeframe." He has to build that analysis manually every time.

### 4. "SOP revisions are where knowledge goes to die"
He writes an SOP revision based on an investigation. The change is technically correct but he can't easily communicate the *why* to operators. The revision goes through document control, gets published, operators read-and-sign, and the context behind the change is lost. Six months later, someone asks "why do we do it this way?" and nobody remembers.

### 5. "Operators stop raising deviations because they think nothing happens"
He sees this constantly. The feedback loop is broken. An operator raises a deviation, Darragh investigates, a CAPA is implemented, the SOP changes — but the operator who raised it never sees the outcome. They think their observation disappeared into a void. So they stop reporting. And then the real problems go undetected.

## Goals
- Spend less time gathering data and more time analysing it
- See patterns across deviations, equipment, and batches without building it manually
- Connect SOP changes back to the investigations that drove them
- Close the feedback loop with operators
- Make the process genuinely better, not just more documented
