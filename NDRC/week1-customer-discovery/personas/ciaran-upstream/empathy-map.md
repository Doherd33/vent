# Empathy Map: Ciaran — Bioprocess Associate (Upstream)

## THINKS
- "If this reactor alarms and I don't catch it, the batch is mine"
- "I know the answer is in the SOP somewhere but I'll be faster just doing what I remember"
- "Why do I have to log into 4 systems to understand one piece of equipment?"
- "The new lad is asking me questions I don't have time to answer right now"
- "I've seen this pH drift before but I can't remember what we did last time"
- "Nobody reads these deviations anyway"
- "I'm not paid enough for how much responsibility I carry on nights"

## FEELS
- **Frustrated** — by tools that slow him down instead of helping him
- **Isolated** — on night shift with no support and high-stakes decisions to make
- **Proud** — of his craft, his ability to run a bioreactor, his process knowledge
- **Anxious** — that one mistake will end up as a deviation with his name on it
- **Undervalued** — he keeps the product alive but the engineers and QA get the credit
- **Resigned** — he's stopped expecting the systems to improve

## SEES
- A gowning room, a cleanroom, bioreactors, control screens, paper batch records
- Colleagues rushing between suites, short-staffed on most shifts
- Senior operators leaving for engineering roles or other companies
- New hires who are "trained" on paper but can't run the process unsupervised
- Management dashboards and KPIs that never reflect what actually happens on the floor
- The same equipment breaking down in the same way, month after month

## HEARS
- "Follow the batch record" — even when the batch record is wrong
- "Raise a deviation" — but never hears the outcome
- "We need to reduce batch cycle times" — but gets no better tools to do it
- "Training is everyone's responsibility" — but there's no time built into the shift for it
- Alarms. Pump alarms, temperature alarms, DO alarms. Constant noise that he's learned to triage by instinct
- Shift handover: 5 minutes of someone talking fast about 12 hours of events

## SAYS
- "Grand, I'll sort it" — even when he's not sure how
- "Has anyone seen this before?" — to colleagues on WhatsApp at 2am
- "The system is brutal" — about the QMS, the MES, all of it
- "I logged it" — meaning he wrote it down, filed it, and expects nothing to happen
- "That's above my pay grade" — when he's pushed to make a decision that should be engineering's call
- "It'd be grand if we just had one place for everything"

## DOES
- Opens the QMS, searches, gives up, asks someone instead
- Writes batch record entries with one eye on the reactor and one on the page
- Checks WhatsApp group for shift handover context that didn't make it to the formal notes
- Takes photos of equipment settings on his phone as a personal backup
- Memorises SOPs rather than looking them up each time
- Stays late after shift to finish paperwork he couldn't complete during the run
- Trains new hires informally, on the floor, because the formal system doesn't prepare them

## PAINS → GAINS (What Vent Shifts)

| Pain | Gain |
|------|------|
| 15 minutes searching for an SOP section | 15 seconds — tap the equipment, ask the question |
| Isolated decisions on night shift | Charlie voice AI as a knowledgeable partner at 3am |
| Knowledge in his head, not in the system | Knowledge grounded to equipment, accessible to everyone |
| Deviations disappear into a void | Full traceability from observation to SOP change |
| "Trained" operators who can't operate | Contextual, equipment-linked training and OJT status |
