# Empathy Map: Eoin — Shift Lead (Manufacturing Operations)

## THINKS
- "What am I not seeing right now?"
- "If I'm in the seed room, who's watching the reactors?"
- "That BPA said they understood but I'm not sure they did"
- "I've seen this alarm pattern before — it was 18 months ago and it led to a deviation"
- "If I raise this deviation now it's 2 hours of paperwork. If I don't, it's worse"
- "The incoming lead isn't going to follow up on this — I need to leave better notes"
- "I'm one phone call away from a very bad night"
- "Why am I the only person on this floor who can answer that question?"
- "I need to be in three places at once and I'm in none of them properly"

## FEELS
- **Burdened** — the weight of the entire shift sits on him. Every mistake is ultimately his
- **Stretched** — too many responsibilities, not enough time, not enough of him to go around
- **Proud** — he's kept this floor running for years. He's good at this and he knows it
- **Lonely** — nobody else on shift truly understands the full picture the way he does
- **Guilty** — when he can't give his BPAs the time and coaching they need
- **Frustrated** — that the tools are decades behind the complexity of the process
- **Hypervigilant** — always scanning, always anticipating, never fully relaxed on shift
- **Underappreciated** — management sees batch output metrics, not the 47 small fires he put out to get there

## SEES
- A whiteboard with shift assignments and task status scrawled in marker
- DeltaV screens in different rooms showing different processes — no unified view
- BPAs standing at batch record stations, writing, looking uncertain
- His phone lighting up with WhatsApp messages from the floor
- Empty chairs where experienced operators used to sit before they left for other companies
- New hires who've been "trained" for 3 weeks and are now running critical processes
- QA emails arriving at 2am asking for documentation he hasn't had time to complete
- The same equipment causing the same problems, month after month

## HEARS
- "Eoin, can you come have a look at this?" — 15 times a shift
- "Is this okay?" — BPAs asking for confirmation they should be able to find themselves
- "We need to reduce headcount" — from management who don't understand what the floor actually requires
- "The deviation rate is too high" — as if raising deviations is the problem, not the equipment
- "I didn't know I was supposed to do that" — from BPAs whose training was a tick-box exercise
- Alarms from three different rooms competing for his attention
- The outgoing shift lead speed-talking through 12 hours of events in 15 minutes
- Silence from engineering and QA after he escalates an issue at midnight

## SAYS
- "Right, what have we got?" — start of every shift, scanning the whiteboard
- "Leave it with me" — taking on problems because it's faster than explaining
- "Did you check the SOP?" — knowing they won't because the QMS is too slow
- "Who's qualified on that?" — before assigning tasks, checking the matrix in his head
- "Log it" — to BPAs, about everything, even when he knows the logging system is painful
- "I'll do the handover notes after" — and sometimes doesn't because the shift ran over
- "Grand" — when it's not grand, but there's no time to explain why
- "We got away with that one" — after a near-miss that should have been a deviation

## DOES
- Walks the floor every 45 minutes — physically checking every active process area
- Keeps a personal notebook with shift notes that's more reliable than any system
- Calls BPAs by name, knows their strengths and weaknesses, assigns tasks accordingly
- Reviews batch record pages in batches of 10, signs them at the end of shift in a rush
- Triages alarms by instinct — knows which ones are real and which are noise
- Makes decisions that should require engineering or QA input because waiting isn't an option
- Texts the incoming shift lead before handover with the critical items
- Stays 30-45 minutes after shift end to finish documentation and handover properly
- Covers for BPAs when they're struggling rather than flagging it — because flagging it means training records, investigations, more paperwork
- Checks his phone on days off because he's worried about what's happening on the floor

## PAINS → GAINS (What Vent Shifts)

| Pain | Gain |
|------|------|
| No single view of the floor | The spatial hub IS the shift overview — every process, every equipment, every status, one screen |
| He's the knowledge bottleneck | Charlie distributes his 11 years of experience to every BPA, every shift, 24/7 |
| Handover drops critical context | Equipment nodes hold state between shifts — nothing falls through because the floor remembers |
| Can't be in three places at once | The floor map shows him what's happening everywhere — he goes where he's actually needed |
| BPAs can't make independent decisions | Vent gives them the SOPs, history, and context to answer their own questions |
| Night shift is unsupported | Charlie + spatial hub = an always-on support system that doesn't go home at 5pm |
| Documentation vs. action tradeoff | Voice-first observation capture — speak it, log it, act. Document properly after |
| Can't develop his team | BPAs who use Vent learn the *why* behind procedures, not just the *what* — they grow faster |

## The Pitch Angle

**Eoin is the demo.**

Show the spatial floor from Eoin's perspective. Show him seeing every active process at a glance. Show a BPA asking Charlie a question instead of calling Eoin. Show the handover context embedded in the equipment nodes. Show the deviation pipeline capturing an observation in 30 seconds instead of 30 minutes.

Then say: *"There are 200 shift leads in Ireland running pharma manufacturing floors like this tonight. Every single one of them is doing it with a whiteboard and a WhatsApp group."*

That's the pitch.
