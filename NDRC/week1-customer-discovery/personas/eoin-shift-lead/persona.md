# Persona: Eoin — Shift Lead (Manufacturing Operations)

## Demographics
- **Role:** Shift Lead — Biomanufacturing Operations
- **Experience:** 11 years in biopharma (started as BPA, promoted through senior operator to shift lead)
- **Employer:** Biopharma CDMO, Ireland
- **Shift pattern:** 12-hour continental shifts (2 days, 2 nights, 4 off) — responsible for 8-12 BPAs per shift

---

## Day-to-Day

Eoin is the single point of accountability for everything that happens on the floor during his shift. He doesn't run equipment himself anymore — he runs the people who run the equipment. His day involves:
- Shift handover (incoming): absorbing 12 hours of context in 15 minutes
- Assigning BPAs to tasks across upstream, downstream, buffer prep, and seed
- Monitoring all active bioreactors, purification runs, and buffer preps simultaneously
- Making go/no-go decisions (is this seed healthy enough to scale? Is this yield acceptable? Do we hold or proceed?)
- Reviewing and co-signing batch record entries
- Troubleshooting when things go wrong — the first call for every problem
- Authorising deviations and ensuring they're documented correctly
- Managing unplanned events (equipment failures, out-of-spec results, staffing gaps)
- Coordinating with QA on-call for critical decisions
- Shift handover (outgoing): compressing 12 hours into 15 minutes for the next lead
- Ensuring training compliance — right people on right equipment

## Frustrations

### 1. "I can't see everything at once"
He's responsible for 3 bioreactors, a downstream purification train, buffer prep, and seed operations — all running simultaneously across different rooms and suites. He has no single view of what's happening. He walks the floor constantly, checks DeltaV screens in different rooms, reads batch records at different stations, and pieces the picture together in his head. If he's in the seed room dealing with a low viability issue, he has no idea that BR-2's pH has been drifting for 20 minutes until someone calls him.

### 2. "I'm the knowledge bottleneck and I hate it"
Every question comes to him. "Is this pH acceptable?" "Can I use this buffer if it's 0.02 off spec?" "The integrity test failed, what do I do?" "Has this happened before?" He knows the answers because he has 11 years of experience — but that means the shift can't function without him. If he's on break, if he's dealing with one crisis, every other question queues up. The team isn't incompetent — they just don't have access to the information they need to make decisions independently.

### 3. "Shift handover is where things fall through the cracks"
Fifteen minutes to transfer 12 hours of context. He tries to cover everything — ongoing batches, pending decisions, equipment issues, QA actions, staffing for the next shift. But he always forgets something, or the incoming lead doesn't absorb it all. The formal handover log is a template that nobody fills in properly. The real handover happens verbally, and verbal information is lossy. Critical context gets dropped between shifts, and problems that should have been flagged get discovered 6 hours too late.

### 4. "I'm signing off on things I haven't witnessed"
Compliance requires his signature on batch record entries. But he can't physically watch every BPA perform every step. He reviews the entries, checks the numbers look right, and signs. If a BPA made an error — wrote the wrong time, missed a step, transcribed a value incorrectly — he might not catch it. He's co-signing based on trust, not verification. That keeps him up at night.

### 5. "I spend more time documenting problems than solving them"
When something goes wrong — and something always goes wrong — his first instinct is to fix it. But compliance demands documentation first. Stop what you're doing, capture the observation, initiate a deviation, record the timeline, notify QA. By the time the paperwork is started, the window for the optimal corrective action may have passed. The system penalises speed and rewards documentation.

### 6. "I can't develop my team because I'm firefighting"
He wants his BPAs to grow — to make decisions independently, to understand the process deeply, to become senior operators. But every shift is firefighting. There's no time for coaching, no time for walkthroughs, no time to explain *why* things are done a certain way. The training system says they're qualified, but he knows they're not ready. And when a BPA makes a mistake because they didn't understand the context, it becomes his deviation to deal with.

### 7. "Night shifts are when I earn my money"
Skeleton crew. Junior BPAs. No engineering support. No QA on-site. Management asleep. And this is when the unusual things happen — the alarms that don't match any SOP, the judgment calls that need experience. He's the only safety net, and if he gets it wrong, it's his name on the deviation.

## Goals
- A single view of the entire shift — every active process, every piece of equipment, every BPA assignment
- Empower his BPAs to answer their own questions so he's not the bottleneck
- Better shift handover — structured, complete, nothing falls through
- Confidence that what's documented is what actually happened
- Time to develop his team instead of just directing them
- Sleep on night shifts without worrying he'll miss something critical
