# Persona: Niamh — Bioprocess Associate (Inoculation / Seed Train)

## Demographics
- **Role:** Bioprocess Associate — Inoculation & Seed Train
- **Experience:** 2 years in biopharma
- **Employer:** Biopharma CDMO, Ireland
- **Shift pattern:** 12-hour continental shifts

---

## Day-to-Day

Niamh manages the earliest and most fragile stage of the bioprocess — taking cells from a vial in the cell bank through shake flasks, wave bags, and small-scale bioreactors up to production inoculation. Her day involves:
- Thawing vials from the cell bank (working cell bank management)
- Aseptic transfers in biosafety cabinets
- Passaging cells through shake flasks and wave bags (2L → 10L → 50L → 200L)
- Monitoring cell viability, VCD, and growth curves
- Making critical go/no-go decisions on whether the seed is healthy enough to scale
- Environmental monitoring (EM) during aseptic operations
- Incubator management and contamination checks

---

## Current Tools & Systems
| System | Her experience |
|--------|----------------|
| Paper batch records | Handwritten entries during aseptic operations — she's writing with gloves on, inside a BSC |
| QMS | SOPs for thaw, passage, and aseptic technique — rarely referenced because she knows them by heart |
| EM monitoring system | Environmental monitoring plates and air samplers — results come back days later |
| Cell bank inventory | Tracked in a spreadsheet or basic database — vial counts, passage numbers, storage locations |
| Vi-CELL / Cedex | Cell counter — gives her VCD and viability but she transcribes results manually |

## Frustrations

### 1. "If I contaminate the seed, there is no batch"
Inoculation is the single point of failure for the entire process. A contamination event here doesn't just affect her — it kills the batch for upstream, downstream, QC, and packaging. Weeks of scheduling gone. She carries this weight every single shift. The pressure is immense and the tools are basic.

### 2. "I'm doing aseptic work and writing on paper at the same time"
She's inside a biosafety cabinet, gowned up, wearing two pairs of gloves, performing aseptic transfers — and she has to stop, pick up a pen, and write in a batch record. Every time she breaks concentration to write, she increases contamination risk. The irony is brutal: the compliance system designed to ensure quality actively undermines the operation it's trying to protect.

### 3. "I have no visibility into the cell bank history"
She thaws a vial and trusts it's good. But what's the passage history? How many vials are left? When was the last time a vial from this lot was thawed and what was the viability? This information exists somewhere — in a different system, in someone's notebook, in a qualification report from 2 years ago. She can't access it when she needs it.

### 4. "Growth curves are in my head"
She knows what a healthy growth curve looks like because she's done it hundreds of times. But if she's off sick and someone covers her shift, they don't have that intuition. There's no system that shows "here's what the last 20 seed trains looked like at this passage — your cells are tracking below normal." That knowledge is entirely experiential.

### 5. "EM results come back too late"
Environmental monitoring results take 3-5 days. If there was a contamination event in the BSC during her inoculation, she won't know until the cells are already in a 200L bioreactor. By then, the damage is done. She has no real-time confidence that her environment was clean.

## Goals
- Protect the seed train at all costs — it's the foundation of everything
- Reduce the need to break aseptic technique for documentation
- Access cell bank history and passage data instantly
- Compare current growth curves to historical data
- Feel supported, not alone, when making go/no-go decisions
