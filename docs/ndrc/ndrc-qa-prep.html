NDRC Q&A Preparation

Anticipated questions and honest answers. Read these, internalise them, but don't memorise word-for-word. Know the substance and say it in your own words.

1. Questions the insider warned you about

What are the compliance and regulatory rules around this? Is it legally complicated to get off the ground?
The short answer is: it's simpler than you'd think, because Vent doesn't replace the regulated system.

In pharma manufacturing, the QMS — Veeva, TrackWise, MasterControl — is the system of record. That's where the regulated data lives. That system needs full GMP validation: IQ/OQ/PQ, 21 CFR Part 11, the lot.

Vent sits upstream of that. It captures the observation, helps with the analysis, and drafts documentation. But the final record still goes into the QMS. Think of it like a drafting tool, not a regulated system.

That said, a pharma site won't use any software without doing due diligence. They'll want to see a validation package, data security, audit trails. I've designed for that from the start — but the formal paperwork is what I need time to complete.
This is a strong answer. It shows you understand the regulatory landscape without overclaiming. If they push further, you can mention 21 CFR Part 11 e-signatures and ALCOA+ data integrity — you've built the architecture for it.
Are large companies automating this internally? Why not?
Some are trying. But internal IT teams build tools for managers, not for operators.

I've seen it firsthand. When a pharma company's IT department builds an internal tool, they build it for the compliance team — dashboards, reports, approvals. Nobody asks the person on the floor what they actually need.

The result is another system that operators have to navigate on top of the five they already use. It doesn't reduce friction — it adds more.

The insight behind Vent isn't technical. It's cultural. I know why people don't report things, because I work alongside them. An internal IT team doesn't have that context.
This is where your domain expertise is your moat. No IT department has spent years on a manufacturing floor watching people avoid the system. You have.
Why has no one built this before?
Two reasons. The AI didn't exist until recently, and the people who understand the problem don't usually build software.

The QMS vendors — Veeva, MasterControl — they're enterprise software companies. They build for the buyer, which is the QA director or the VP of Quality. They've never worked on a manufacturing floor.

The people who actually see the problem every day — operators, process associates — they don't typically have the technical background to build a solution. I happen to have both. Immunology degree, working on the floor daily, and I've taught myself to build with AI tools.

That combination is unusual. That's why it hasn't been done.
How big can this business be?
The pharma QMS market is $3 billion and growing at 13% a year. But honestly, I'm not focused on the total market right now.

There are roughly 300 biopharmaceutical manufacturing sites in Ireland alone. 9 of the top 10 global pharma companies have manufacturing here. Each site is a potential customer.

If Vent works at one site, the playbook is to expand site-by-site within that company, then company-by-company within Ireland, then internationally. Pharma is global but the processes are remarkably standardised — what works in Cork works in Boston.

I don't know exactly how big this gets. But the problem is real, the market is large, and it starts with one site.
"I don't know exactly how big this gets" is a good line. It's honest. Follow it immediately with "but the problem is real" so it doesn't sound uncertain.
What is your strategy for selling to pharma companies? Do you have connections to get in the door?
I work inside a major CDMO. I'm already in the building.

My first target is a pilot at a site I can access directly — either my own facility or one of the multinationals nearby. Pfizer, Lilly, MSD, Regeneron all have manufacturing within two hours of me.

Pharma sales are long cycles — 6 to 18 months typically. But I'm not starting cold. I'm starting as someone who works on the floor, who understands the problem, and who can demo a working prototype to a QA director over coffee. That's a very different conversation to a cold sales email.

After the first pilot, the sell becomes easier because you have a reference site and real data.

2. The solo founder questions

You're a solo founder with no technical co-founder. Who's going to build the production version?
I built the prototype myself — that's what I can show you today.

I used AI-assisted development tools and I've gotten surprisingly far. The SOP search works. The GDP checker works. The interface is functional.

But I'm honest — to take this to enterprise-grade, production-ready software, I need a technical co-founder. Someone with experience scaling SaaS products. That's one of the three things I'm here to get help with.

I don't think the programming is the hard part. Understanding the problem deeply enough to build the right thing — that's the hard part. And I have that.
They will almost certainly ask this. Don't get defensive. Own it: "I need help scaling it. That's why I'm here." Confidence in what you've built, honesty about what you need.
How long have you been working on this?
A few weeks.

I know that sounds early. But look at what exists — a working SOP search, a GDP documentation tool, a prototype that real operators have used and given feedback on. I've moved fast because I understand the problem inside out and the AI development tools available today are genuinely powerful.

I've been thinking about this problem for years. I've been building for weeks.
"Thinking about it for years, building for weeks" — use this line. It reframes the timeline from "just started" to "finally acting on something I've known for ages."
What makes you the right person to do this?
I have the domain expertise, I understand the science, and I've proven I can build.

I studied Immunology at Trinity. I work in biopharmaceutical manufacturing every day. I understand the SOPs, the deviations, the CAPAs, the culture on the floor. That's not something you can hire for easily.

On the technical side — I'm not a software engineer, but I've built a working prototype that real people are using and giving feedback on. I've learned fast and I'll keep learning.

On the sales side — I work inside the industry. I know the people, I know the pain points, and I know how to talk to a QA director about their problems because I live them.
Will you stick at this? You haven't been doing it long.
I'm working evenings and weekends on top of a full-time job on a manufacturing floor. I wouldn't be doing that if I wasn't serious.

I'm here asking for help to leave a stable, well-paying job to pursue this. That's not a decision I take lightly. But every shift I work, I see the problem. And now I know it can be solved.

The speed I've moved at should tell you something about my commitment. I'm not testing the water. I'm in.

3. Product and market questions

Are these paying customers?
No. This is feedback from colleagues at my facility.

I work with these people. I showed them the prototype and asked what they thought. They weren't being polite — they were telling me what they'd actually use on the floor.

Getting a formal pilot with a paying customer is exactly what I need time and support to do. I can't run a sales process while working full-time shifts.
Don't try to make informal feedback sound like customer validation. Just be honest. "These are colleagues, not customers. Getting to customers is why I'm here." That's a perfectly good answer at pre-accelerator stage.
What happens if Veeva or MasterControl adds this functionality?
They could, but they probably won't — and here's why.

Veeva and MasterControl are built around compliance workflows. Their entire architecture, their user base, their sales motion — it's all aimed at QA departments. Building an operator-facing, low-friction reporting tool is a fundamentally different product for a fundamentally different user.

It's like asking why Salesforce didn't build Slack. They could have. But it's not what their product does or who their user is.

And even if they tried, they'd build it as an add-on to their existing system. It would still feel like a QMS tool. The whole point of Vent is that it doesn't.
How do you protect the IP? What's the moat?
The moat isn't the technology. It's the domain knowledge embedded in the product.

Anyone can build an AI tool. But knowing which SOPs matter for which observations, understanding how a CAPA should be structured for a biologics facility, knowing what a training lead needs versus what an operator needs — that comes from working on the floor.

Over time, the moat deepens: every site that uses Vent generates data about their specific deviation patterns, their SOPs, their processes. That becomes a flywheel that's very hard to replicate.
What's the pricing model?
I haven't validated pricing yet. But the direction is SaaS, per site, annual licence.

Current QMS tools charge anywhere from €60K to €480K per site per year. I think Vent sits well below that — maybe €35K to €60K per site — but I'd need to validate that with actual buyers.

The ROI case is straightforward: if a site runs 30 to 100 CAPAs a year, each taking 40 to 60 man-hours, even a 30% reduction in investigation time saves more than the licence costs in labour alone.
It's fine to not have validated pricing. Say "I haven't validated it yet" — that's honest at this stage. Then give the directional range so they know you've thought about it.
Can you give us a demo?
Yes — I can show the SOP search working live and walk through the GDP checker.

The SOP search is the strongest feature right now. I can search for a process step in plain language and get the right section of the right document. It works against real documentation.

The CAPA generation and risk classification — I can show you what the output looks like, but I'd be honest that it's early. The prompts work but it needs more testing before I'd trust it in a production environment.
If they ask for a demo, only show what actually works. SOP search live, GDP checker if it's behaving. Walk through screenshots or outputs for the CAPA stuff. Don't try to demo something that might break.

4. Quick-fire answers

What's your runway?
I'm still employed full-time. I have no burn rate. But I also have no time — that's the constraint, not money.
What do you need from NDRC specifically?
Time, mentorship, and introductions. Time to go full-time. Mentorship on the go-to-market and GMP validation pathway. Introductions to people who've sold enterprise software to pharma.
What's your timeline for a first pilot?
If I can go full-time, I'd want to have a pilot site lined up within 3 months and running within 6. The product is close enough that the main work is sales and validation, not development.
What's the biggest risk?
Pharma sales cycles. The problem is real, the product works, but getting a regulated facility to formally adopt new software takes time. That's why starting with a pilot — even informal — matters so much.
Are you willing to leave your job?
Yes. That's literally what I'm asking for help with. I'm ready to go.
