NDRC Pitch Script

3-minute showcase pitch (7 slides). ~350 words. Read it, practise it a few times, then put it down and say it in your own words. Don't memorise it word-for-word.
1-Minute Introductory Pitch (no slides — memorise the gist)

Hi, I'm Darren. I work as a Bioprocess Associate at a large biopharmaceutical manufacturer in Ireland.

The problem I see every day: the documentation on a pharma floor doesn't always match the actual process. And when an operator spots a mismatch, the reporting process is so slow and painful that most of the time, people just don't bother. The same issue happens shift after shift before anyone connects the dots. Each batch failure costs one to five million euro.

Vent makes it easy for operators to flag things — 30 seconds, plain language, any device. AI helps with the documentation burden that currently takes QA teams weeks.

I've built a working prototype and I've tested it with real operators on the floor. They want to use it. I need to leave the floor and build this full-time.

~120 words. Under 1 minute at a natural speaking pace. Practise with a timer.
1
Who I Am
~20 seconds

Hi everyone, I'm Darren Doherty. I work as a Bioprocess Associate at a large biopharmaceutical CDMO in Ireland.

I studied Immunology at Trinity. I understand the science and I understand the manufacturing — because I do it every day.

I haven't stopped building since.

Keep it short. You're just establishing: I work on a pharma floor, I studied science, I built a thing. 20 seconds max.
2
A Real Example — The PBR Mismatch
~40 seconds

Let me show you a real example from the floor.

This is a step from a Process Batch Record — a PBR. It's the document operators follow for every batch.

This step says: flush the ATF filter with 100 litres of WFI. But anyone who's actually done it knows 100 litres isn't enough for the tubing length on that skid. The SOP says 150 litres.

That ATF filter costs around fifty thousand euro. If the flush is wrong, you risk damaging it — or worse, contaminating the batch. A batch failure in biologics is one to five million euro.

The documentation doesn't match the process. And the operator on the floor can see it. PAUSE This happens more than you'd think.

This is your 'show don't tell' moment. Point at the PBR table on screen. Point at the 100L. Say "but the SOP says 150." Don't rush the filter cost. Let the numbers land. You LIVE this — speak from experience.
3
What Happens Today
~30 seconds

So what happens when an operator sees this? Here's the current process.

You tell your supervisor. Your supervisor tells their manager. The manager raises it with the site lead. It goes into an Excel sheet. And then... it waits. PAUSE Until the end-of-batch review. Maybe weeks later.

Sometimes the change gets made. Sometimes it gets lost. And the next operator runs the same wrong step.

The systems for catching this stuff exist. They're just so painful that people work around them.

Walk through the chain on screen left to right. Point at each step. The pause before "Until the end-of-batch review" is important — let the silence do the work. This isn't about anonymity or fear. It's about friction.
4
Vent SOP Search — Finding the Mismatch
~30 seconds

Now let me show you what this looks like with Vent.

Same situation. You're on the floor. The PBR says 100 litres. Something feels wrong.

You open Vent. You search in plain language: "What WFI volume is needed for ATF filter flush?" PAUSE

In seconds, it pulls up the relevant SOP section and shows you: 150 litres. The mismatch is flagged automatically.

This feature works today. I've tested it with real operators. One lad on the buffer team searched for a process step, got exactly what he needed, and couldn't believe it was that simple.

Point at the search bar. Point at the result. Point at the red "MISMATCH DETECTED" tag. This is your strongest feature — it WORKS. Don't oversell it. Just show it and let it land.
5
Raise a Concern — Floor to QA in 30 Seconds
~30 seconds

Now the operator clicks "Raise a Concern." They describe what they found in plain language. 30 seconds. Done. They're back to work.

On the other side, QA Document Control sees this. They get a clear diff — the PBR says 100L, the SOP says 150L — with the supporting documentation already pulled.

They can review it, approve the change, and push it into the next PBR revision.

What used to take weeks of verbal handoffs now takes minutes.

Walk through the left side (operator form) then the right side (QA view). Point at the diff view — that's the visual money shot. This feature is EARLY — the concept works but it's not production-ready. Be honest if they ask. Say "the concept is proven" not "it's finished."
6
Early Feedback + Market
~35 seconds

I've been showing this to people on the floor.

The training lead said it would bring their department into the 21st century. A buffer operator searched for a process step and couldn't believe how easy it was. An operator with dyslexia found the GDP checker genuinely useful — that was a need I hadn't planned for.

The pharma QMS market is three billion dollars, growing at 13% a year. The current tools cost hundreds of thousands per year. They're built for compliance managers, not for operators.

Vent is the layer underneath — capturing what's actually happening before it reaches the system.

Hit the three stories fast — one sentence each. The training lead quote is your strongest, lean into it. On market: move fast. Don't dwell on numbers. If they want more detail, that's what Q&A is for. Be honest: "these are colleagues, not customers."
7
The Ask
~25 seconds

I need to take this from a prototype to a real pilot.

First — a pharma site in Ireland. I work inside the industry. Pfizer, Lilly, MSD, Regeneron are all within two hours. These are doors I can get into.

Second — I need help navigating the GMP validation pathway. A regulated facility won't use software without formal documentation.

Third — eventually, a technical co-founder. Someone who's scaled enterprise SaaS. I can build the prototype. I need someone who knows how to scale it.

PAUSE

The people on the floor are already telling me they want this. I just need the time to make it happen.

Be direct. Don't ramble after "make it happen" — that's your last line. Let it sit. If there's a silence, that's fine. They'll start the Q&A.
